About

The mathematician & the project

René Thom

René Frédéric Thom was a French mathematician whose work permanently altered the way science thinks about form, stability, and change. Educated at the École Normale Supérieure, he received the Fields Medal in 1958 for his foundational contributions to differential topology, particularly his development of cobordism theory.

His major work, Structural Stability and Morphogenesis (1972), proposed that the universe's tendency to produce persistent forms — organisms, waves, social structures, flames — could be understood through a classification of smooth potential landscapes near their degenerate critical points. He proved that up to a certain dimensional complexity, only seven qualitatively distinct types of instability are possible. He called them the elementary catastrophes.

Thom spent most of his career at the Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques in Bures-sur-Yvette. He was controversial — catastrophe theory attracted both deep admirers and sharp critics — but the mathematics was never in dispute. The classification is a theorem, not a metaphor.

1923 – 2002 · Fields Medal 1958 · IHÉS
Andrea Cortis, PhD

Andrea Cortis is a researcher and writer working at the intersection of mathematics, philosophy, and theology. His work investigates the deep structures underlying identity, boundary, and form — the conditions under which a thing remains itself and the topologies of its possible collapse.

His forthcoming monograph, The Taxonomy of Attractors, applies Thom's catastrophe theory to the cross-cultural convergence of serious philosophical and theological inquiry — tracing how independent traditions arrive at the same small set of stable positions, and mapping the characteristic errors available from each.

He is also the author of the book The Erosion of Boundaries.
